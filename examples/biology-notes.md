# Biology Notes: Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process plants, algae, and some bacteria use to convert light energy into chemical energy. The chemical energy is stored in sugars such as glucose. This process supports most food chains on Earth.

In plants, photosynthesis happens mainly in chloroplasts. Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, a green pigment that absorbs light. Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light strongly and reflects more green light, which is why many leaves look green.

The overall photosynthesis equation is carbon dioxide plus water plus light energy producing glucose and oxygen. Carbon dioxide enters leaves through small openings called stomata. Water is absorbed by roots and transported through the xylem.

The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts. These reactions capture light energy and use it to produce ATP and NADPH. Oxygen is released when water molecules are split.

The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. It uses carbon dioxide, ATP, and NADPH to build sugar molecules. The Calvin cycle does not directly require light, but it depends on products from the light-dependent reactions.

Stomata can open and close to regulate gas exchange. When stomata open, carbon dioxide can enter and oxygen can leave. However, open stomata also allow water vapor to escape, so plants must balance photosynthesis with water conservation.

Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are connected but opposite in important ways. Photosynthesis stores energy in glucose and releases oxygen. Cellular respiration breaks down glucose to release usable energy and produces carbon dioxide and water.

Light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, and temperature can affect the rate of photosynthesis. If one factor is too low or too high, it can limit the overall rate. Very high temperatures can reduce photosynthesis by disrupting enzymes.

Plants use glucose for energy, growth, and storage. Some glucose is converted into starch for later use. Glucose can also be used to build cellulose, which strengthens plant cell walls.

Photosynthesis is important for the atmosphere because it releases oxygen and removes carbon dioxide. Over long periods, photosynthetic organisms helped make Earth's atmosphere oxygen-rich. Modern ecosystems still depend on photosynthesis as a primary source of energy.
